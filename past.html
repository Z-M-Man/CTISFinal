Naval Warfare in the Past

The first warships were used by Ancient Egypt. The galley, a long and narrow ship driven by oars, was invented in Crete and became the only type of warship used by many ancient civilizations. The primary strategy of galleys was to either ram and sink enemy ships or get close enough to board and fight the enemy crew. The galleys could also double as merchant ships when not used for fighting. In the 4th century BC, the invention of catapults led some ships to be equipped with seige engines--a precursor to the cannon ship. Around the late 3rd century, ramming became less popular as a tactic and was eventually abandoned.

The invention of cannons and other gunpowder guns was, at first, beneficial for galleys. Early cannons had a very short effective range and a long reload, which allowed the fast galley to close the distance and fire a lethal shot before the enemy could reload in time. Boarding still remained one of the main strategies, especially in large fleet battles where the galleys would inevitably run into each other.

In the 15th and 16th century, cannons began to be installed in greater numbers and on the side of ships. However, shortcomings in the cannons themselves meant that galleys still dominated the seas. This changed with the development of the man-of-war, a large ship heavily armed with cannons. To accomodate for the extra weight, the cannons were moved lower into the decks that would normally hold cargo. Not only did this make them more dangerous to the smaller galleys, it also meant that the man-of-war was completely dedicated to combat and could not be used for merchant activities outside of war, a first in warships. Thus, ships began to be built exclusively as warships, and the galley was slowly replaced by the man-of-war as the primary ship of navies.
